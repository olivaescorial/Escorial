During the Spanish and American colonial periods in the Philippines, elections were initially used. However, the process was only open to male, educated,
and landowner voters, and it was more of a ceremonial than a genuine democratic system. Sectoral resistance and political engagement were only fulfilled in 1937, when Act 4112, guaranteeing women the right to vote,
was passed, and peasant movements acquired genuine participation in political parties and government offices. The 1946 election for President
and Vice-President of the Republic, as well as members of the Senate
and House of Representatives, was the first democratic election following
WWII. From then on, the country ran under a two-party system, with the Nacionalista Party and the Liberal Party voting for power in subsequent
elections.

The 1935 Constitution was scrapped when Marcos proclaimed martial law in 1972. An Interim Batasang Pambansa was formed, and a new constitution (1973) was adopted. Under transition rules, Marcos reigned as both president and prime minister, with legislative powers—a one-man dictatorship.
